Negative Harmony Machine

Play a chord progression. Hear its mirror.

Pick a key, choose a chord type, then click any piano key to build a chord on that root. Right after pressing a note, hit "Add chord" to lock it into your chord — the mirror column updates automatically based on the selected key, i.e. it's a mirror of the entire progression, not on a chord by chord basis. You can repeat this process to add more chords. The Tonnetz visualization will show the notes of the original and mirrored chord.

Why I built it

Negative harmony is one of those music theory concepts that's easy to describe and hard to feel. You can read the rule — "reflect every pitch over an axis between the tonic and the fifth" — and still have no idea what it's actually going to sound like until you hear it. The classic Jacob Collier demos and live sessions detail this well, and I thought it'd be an interesting project to implemented this visually for all to see.

The Negative Harmony Machine is a web app where you input a chord progression on a piano keyboard, hear it played back through a WebAudio synth, and simultaneously see and hear its negative harmony transformation rendered on a Tonnetz lattice. The goal was to make a piece of math (the TnI inversion operation from pitch-class set theory) into something you can actually play with.

The math, briefly

Negative harmony is just pitch inversion around an axis. If you label the 12 pitch classes as integers 0 through 11 (C=0, C#=1, … B=11), and define an axis based on the tonic of your key — specifically axis = (tonic + 7) % 12, which sits halfway between the tonic and its perfect fifth — then for any note, its mirror is (axis - note + 12) % 12.

That's it. The whole transformation is one line of code. Apply it to every note in a chord and you get the mirror chord. Apply it to every chord in a progression and you get the negative harmony version of the whole piece. The reason this produces musically coherent results rather than random noise is that the reflection preserves interval structure — a major third becomes a minor third, a perfect fifth stays a perfect fifth, and the internal "shape" of each chord is mirrored rather than scrambled. It's the same TnI operation from set theory, applied with musical intent.

Architecture

The codebase is split into four ES modules with a deliberately tight separation of concerns:

  • theory.js — pure math. Pitch class definitions, chord interval tables, the TnI inversion function, neo-Riemannian P/L/R operations, chord-name reverse lookup.
  • audio.js — the WebAudio engine. AudioContext lifecycle, oscillator + gain wiring, ADSR envelope shaping, chord playback, the step sequencer that drives progression playback.
  • tonnetz.js — canvas rendering. A class that owns the canvas element, generates the lattice's node positions and edges, and exposes a single update() method for changing what's highlighted.
  • app.js — Owns the DOM, builds the piano keyboard, wires UI events to the other three modules, manages user-facing state.

The first three modules know nothing about the others. theory.js doesn't know audio exists. audio.js imports a couple of frequency conversion helpers from theory but never touches the DOM. tonnetz.js imports pitch class names and is otherwise self-contained. All the coordination happens in app.js, which made debugging much easier — a bug was almost always traceable to one specific module.

The audio engine

Sound generation uses oscillator-based additive synthesis. Each note in a chord gets its own OscillatorNode connected to a GainNode that shapes its ADSR envelope. The gain nodes feed into a per-chord master, and all per-chord masters feed into a global master gain bus before reaching audioContext.destination.

The single global master was added late after I noticed that playBoth mode — which stacks the original and mirror progressions on top of each other — was clipping badly. With up to eight oscillators going at once, even with per-chord normalization, the summed signal was exceeding 0dBFS at the destination. Routing everything through one master gain at 0.4 gave the signal chain enough headroom to handle the worst case cleanly.

Stopping mid-playback also produces clicks if you just call osc.stop() while the gain node is still at full sustain. The clean stop pattern is: cancel any scheduled gain values, take a sample of the current gain, ramp it to zero over a short interval (I used 80ms), then stop the oscillator just after the ramp completes.

The Tonnetz renderer

The Tonnetz is a 2D lattice in which every node is a pitch class, every triangle is a triad, and physical proximity corresponds to harmonic relationship. Moving right one node is up a perfect fifth. Moving up and to the right is up a major third. Moving up and to the left is up a minor third. Every upward-pointing triangle is a major triad, every downward-pointing triangle is a minor triad.

For rendering, I generate node positions procedurally rather than hand-placing them. Each node has a (row, col) and its pitch class is computed as ((col * 7) + (row * 3)) % 12 — fifths going right, minor thirds going up. The visual stagger of every other row (offsetting x by half a step) gives the lattice its hexagonal feel. Edges are built by walking each node and connecting to its right, down-right, and down-left neighbors if they exist.

The reflection axis is drawn as a vertical dashed line. Rather than hardcoding its x position, I find all nodes whose pitch class matches the axis value and average their x coordinates — so the line automatically follows the axis when the key changes, with no separate state to keep in sync.

Interaction design

The biggest design question was how to let users input chords. My first version had a piano keyboard where you'd click and hold multiple keys to build a chord, then click "Add chord" while still holding — which turned out to be impossible with a mouse, since mouseleave fires when you move toward the button. I tried a few workarounds (click-to-toggle, computer keyboard mapping) and ultimately landed on a chord type selector: pick a quality (major, minor, dom7, half-diminished, etc.), then clicking any piano key plays the full chord with that note as the root. This is faster than note-by-note input, sufficient for the kinds of progressions people actually want to test, and doesn't require any modifier keys or sustain logic.

Reverse chord naming (mapping a set of pitch classes back to a chord symbol like "F minor" or "D half-diminished 7th") is done by brute force — for each chord type in my dictionary, try all 12 possible roots and check if any combination produces the input set. With 12 chord types and 12 roots, that's 144 set-equality checks at worst, which is fine for a 3-7 note input.

What I learned

If I were going to keep developing this, the next step would be melody input — letting users sing or type a melodic line and hearing its negative harmony version note by note rather than just chord by chord. Melodies expose the inversion more nakedly than harmony does, because there's no chordal context to absorb the strangeness. I'd also love to add a tonnetz "flip" animation, where the lattice physically rotates around the axis when you toggle between original and mirror modes. There are other visual changes and finicky things to worry about, but the overall project should be straight-forward to use
